# AI Rollups

## The Core Argument

A new investment thesis is emerging: acquire traditional businesses at legacy multiples, implement AI-driven efficiency gains, and exit at transformed valuations. This isn't speculative—major funds are already deploying billions.

> **Key Claim:** The arbitrage between traditional business valuations and AI-native multiples creates a 24-36 month window for outsized returns.

General Catalyst has dedicated $1.5B specifically to this strategy. They're not alone. The playbook is straightforward: find labor-intensive businesses with repeatable processes, acquire at 4-6x EBITDA, implement AI to cut costs 30-50%, and exit at 8-12x as an "AI-transformed" company.

## Why This Works Now

Three forces converge to make this window possible:

**1. Valuation Gap**
Traditional services businesses trade at 4-8x EBITDA. AI-native companies command 15-30x. The delta between these multiples represents the value creation opportunity—if you can credibly transform the business.

**2. AI Capabilities Have Arrived**
Until 2023, "AI transformation" was mostly marketing. Now the tools actually work. Document processing, customer service, code generation, research synthesis—these aren't experiments anymore. They're deployable at scale.

**3. Talent Arbitrage**
The best AI implementers (Knowledge Programmers) are still relatively cheap compared to the value they create. A $200K hire who can 10x a team's productivity is absurdly underpriced. This won't last.

## The Target Profile

Not every business is a candidate. The ideal targets share characteristics:

- **Labor-intensive operations**: 40%+ of costs in people doing repeatable cognitive work
- **Process-driven delivery**: Consulting, accounting, legal support, content production, customer service
- **Fragmented industry**: Many small players, no dominant AI-native competitor yet
- **Founder fatigue**: Owners in their 50s-60s looking for exits, not transformations
- **$5-50M revenue**: Big enough to matter, small enough to transform quickly

> "The best deals are businesses that are successful despite their inefficiency—lots of room to improve without breaking what works."

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Acquire (Months 0-3)
- Buy at traditional multiples (4-6x EBITDA for services)
- Retain key client relationships during transition
- Assess which processes are automation candidates

### Phase 2: Transform (Months 3-12)
- Hire 2-3 Knowledge Programmers as transformation leads
- Implement AI tooling for highest-volume, most repeatable work first
- Target 30-40% efficiency gains in first 12 months
- Reinvest some savings into growth, bank the rest as margin

### Phase 3: Scale (Months 12-24)
- Document the AI-augmented operating model
- Hire from the "new" talent pool (AI-fluent, lower cost)
- Expand into adjacent services now economical to offer
- Build case studies showing transformation metrics

### Phase 4: Exit (Months 18-36)
- Position as AI-native services platform
- Target strategic acquirers or PE firms building AI portfolios
- Exit at 2-3x entry multiple if transformation succeeded

## The Math

Consider a $20M revenue services business:
- **Acquisition**: $20M revenue, 15% EBITDA ($3M), bought at 5x = $15M
- **Transformation**: AI reduces labor costs 35%, EBITDA rises to 28% ($5.6M)
- **Growth**: Revenue grows 20% to $24M, EBITDA = $6.7M
- **Exit**: Sold at 8x transformed EBITDA = $54M
- **Return**: 3.6x in 24-30 months

This is the bull case. Reality involves integration failures, client churn, transformation friction. But even half this return beats most alternatives.

## The Risks

**Execution Risk**
AI transformation isn't automatic. Many businesses will fail to implement effectively. The delta between "bought an AI tool" and "transformed operations" is vast.

**Multiple Compression**
If everyone piles into this strategy, entry multiples rise and exit multiples fall. The arbitrage closes. First movers win; late entrants compete for scraps.

**Talent Scarcity**
The Knowledge Programmers who can actually execute transformations are rare. Overpaying for mediocre implementation talent is a common failure mode.

**Client Exodus**
Clients may not want "AI-powered" service delivery. Some will churn. The transformation must happen fast enough to capture savings before revenue erodes.

## Who's Playing

- **General Catalyst**: $1.5B dedicated fund for AI services transformation
- **Vista Equity Partners**: Already applies operational playbook; AI is natural extension
- **Thoma Bravo**: Software focus, but services adjacencies emerging
- **Search funds**: Individual operators targeting single businesses for AI transformation

The field is getting crowded. The window is real, but it's not infinite.

## Investment Implications

For investors:
- Early-stage AI rollup funds offer highest potential returns with highest risk
- Later-stage funds will pay higher multiples but face compressed exits
- Direct co-investment in specific deals can provide better terms

For operators:
- Building AI transformation capabilities is itself a valuable skill
- "AI transformation as a service" for PE portfolios is an emerging opportunity
- The playbook that works becomes the template everyone wants

> "The time to enter is now. In 36 months, the arbitrage will have largely closed."

## Connection to Knowledge Work Thesis

AI Rollups is the investment expression of the AI Knowledge Work thesis. If knowledge work economics are inverting, businesses built on old economics become transformation targets. The same forces that make Knowledge Programmers valuable make traditional services businesses undervalued.

These theses reinforce each other: evidence of AI productivity gains validates the rollup strategy; successful rollups prove the productivity thesis is real.
